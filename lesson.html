1 — Special Triangles

Two special right triangles give us all the exact trig values on the unit circle. Every angle on the circle is just one of these triangles placed in a quadrant.

30 – 60 – 90 Triangle
45 – 45 – 90 Triangle

Quick Reference

Triangle Angles Short Leg Long Leg Hypotenuse Origin
30-60-90 30°, 60°, 90° 1 √3 2 Half equilateral (side 2)
45-45-90 45°, 45°, 90° 1 1 √2 Diagonal of unit square

Why these triangles?

On the unit circle the hypotenuse is always $r = 1$. Dividing every side of the 30-60-90 triangle by 2 gives sides $\dfrac{1}{2}$, $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$, $1$. Those are exactly the $\sin$ and $\cos$ values at $30^{\circ}$ and $60^{\circ}$. Dividing the 45-45-90 triangle by $\sqrt{2}$ gives sides $\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$, $\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$, $1$ — the values at $45^{\circ}$.

2 — SOHCAHTOA → Unit Circle

SOHCAHTOA is the mnemonic for the three basic trig ratios in a right triangle. When the hypotenuse equals 1 (the unit circle radius), the ratios simplify beautifully.

SOH
sin θ = Opposite / Hypotenuse
CAH
cos θ = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
TOA
tan θ = Opposite / Adjacent
When r = 1 (unit circle): sin θ = y / 1 = y cos θ = x / 1 = x tan θ = y / x
Unit Circle Right Triangle
sin θ = y → the y-coordinate of a point on the unit circle cos θ = x → the x-coordinate of a point on the unit circle tan θ = y / x → slope of the radius line from origin to P csc θ = 1/y → reciprocal of sin sec θ = 1/x → reciprocal of cos cot θ = x/y → reciprocal of tan

Key insight

Every point on the unit circle can be written as $(\cos\theta,\, \sin\theta)$. The angle $\theta$ is measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis. This is why memorizing the coordinates is memorizing the trig values.

4 — Trig Graphs

All six trig functions are periodic. Understanding their graphs helps predict behavior and spot identities.

Key graph properties

  • $\sin$ & $\cos$: period $2\pi$, range $[-1, 1]$, no asymptotes
  • $\tan$: period $\pi$, vertical asymptotes at $x = \dfrac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$
  • csc: reciprocal of sin — asymptotes where sin = 0
  • sec: reciprocal of cos — asymptotes where cos = 0
  • $\cot$: period $\pi$, asymptotes where $\sin = 0$

5 — Radians vs Degrees

What is a radian?

A radian is the angle whose arc length equals the radius. When you travel along the circle a distance equal to $r$, you've swept through exactly $1$ radian.

Arc Length = Radius = 1 Radian
360° = 2π radians 180° = π radians 1° = π/180 radians 1 rad = 180°/π ≈ 57.3° Convert deg → rad: multiply by π/180 Convert rad → deg: multiply by 180/π

Coterminal Angles

Coterminal angles share the same terminal side — they point in the same direction but differ by full rotations of $360^{\circ}$ $(2\pi)$.

Coterminal with θ: θ + 360°·n (degrees) θ + 2π·n (radians) where n is any integer (positive or negative)

Examples:

$-30^{\circ} \equiv 330^{\circ}$ $390^{\circ} \equiv 30^{\circ}$ $-\dfrac{\pi}{4} \equiv \dfrac{7\pi}{4}$ $\dfrac{5\pi}{2} \equiv \dfrac{\pi}{2}$ $720^{\circ} \equiv 0^{\circ}$ $-270^{\circ} \equiv 90^{\circ}$

Reference Angles

The reference angle is the acute angle (≤ 90°) between the terminal side and the x-axis. It determines the magnitude of trig values — the quadrant determines the sign.

Q1: ref = θ Q2: ref = 180° − θ Q3: ref = θ − 180° Q4: ref = 360° − θ
